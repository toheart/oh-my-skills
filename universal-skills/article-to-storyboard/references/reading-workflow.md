# Reading Workflow

## Purpose

Use this reference when the input is still a long article, rough notes, or a dense argument that has not yet been shaped for video.

## Step 1: Extract the Core Thesis

Find the one sentence that best answers:

- What is the author really arguing?
- What should the viewer remember after the video ends?

If the piece contains multiple claims, decide which one is primary and which ones are supporting.

## Step 2: Identify the Audience

A storyboard should reflect who the video is for.
Infer or ask:

- who the intended viewer is
- how much background they already have
- whether the tone should be explanatory, editorial, persuasive, or reflective

## Step 3: Map the Argument Structure

Break the source into functional units, not just paragraphs.
Useful categories:

- hook
- thesis
- evidence
- example
- contrast
- process
- implication
- conclusion

This step is more important than preserving the original order exactly.

## Step 4: Decide What to Compress

Most written material is too dense for video.
Cut:

- repeated phrasing
- caveats that do not change the argument
- examples that duplicate the same point
- transitions that are helpful in writing but dead in video

Keep:

- core claim
- strongest evidence
- most illustrative example
- emotional or conceptual turning points

## Step 5: Convert to Watching Beats

Turn the argument into a sequence that can be seen and heard.
A useful default flow is:

1. hook
2. thesis
3. explanation
4. evidence
5. consequence or contrast
6. conclusion

Not every source needs all six, but most strong short videos need a comparable arc.

## Step 6: Mark Source Traceability

When fidelity matters, assign each beat a source reference such as:

- paragraph numbers
- bullet identifiers
- section headings
- transcript timestamps if available

This makes later review much easier and reduces drift.
